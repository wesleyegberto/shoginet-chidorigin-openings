Demon Killer (Oni-goroshi) Strategy [1]

I will introduce a somewhat suspicious strategy said to be so powerful that "if you jump the Knight on the third move, you can defeat even a warrior as strong as an ogre." I will also explain the correct way to respond to it.

At the very moment both sides open their Bishop diagonals with ▲7六歩 ▽3四歩, Sente immediately jumps the Knight with ▲7七桂. This is the ancient hame-te (trap) strategy known as "Oni-goroshi" (Demon Killer). Despite the proverb "a high-jumping Knight is prey for a Pawn," Sente pays no heed and charges forward with ▲6五桂. When Gote plays ▽6四歩 to trap the Knight, Sente exchanges Bishops and drops ▲5五角.

The subsequent attacking line resembles the Quick Ishida from Chapter 16 but with even greater intensity. By Figure 1, the position is so favorable for Sente that one could call it a win. The added presence of the Knight compared to the standard Quick Ishida makes the offensive power truly formidable.

Up to this point, it doesn't seem like Gote made any obviously bad moves, yet they were left completely helpless. This is precisely why it is said to defeat even an "ogre."

In truth, moves that are normally considered good shape are actually mistakes specifically against the Demon Killer. The problem lies with a move we've seen frequently: ▽6二銀. In this specific scenario, ▽6二金 is the correct response. Now, we will see how to "kill" the Demon Killer.

Following the same sequence as leading to Figure 1, Gote moves ▽6二金 when Sente jumps ▲6五桂. With this single move, the Demon Killer strategy is easily crushed. When Sente uses the same response to ▽6四歩 by exchanging Bishops and dropping ▲5五角, Gote now plays ▽6三金. This move protects the pawn at 6-4 while simultaneously allowing the Rook to support the Silver at 2-2. By defending both threats at once, Sente's attack is exhausted (sashikiri) and ends in failure.

In the previous case with ▽6二銀, the piece at 6-3 in Figure 2 would be a Silver, which would allow Sente to play ▲5三桂成, leading to a disadvantage for Gote.

This is the characteristic of a hame-te (trap): if the opponent does not fall for the trap and defends accurately, the position becomes impossible to salvage. In other words, if the opponent isn't caught, the player has no way to transition to another strategy and simply collapses.

Usually, introductory books and standard theory (joseki) texts would end the explanation of the Demon Killer here. However, I believe that is insufficient. This is because there is a sequence that counter-traps the very defense commonly used to "shut down" the Demon Killer. I will explain that in the next chapter.
