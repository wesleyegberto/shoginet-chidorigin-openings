「図１」


"Figure 1" shows the position where Gote has retreated the Rook with ▽８二飛 in response to ▲７七桂, focusing on organizing their camp. Gote's formation with ▽３二金 and ▽４二銀 is known as "Kanemino" (Gold Mino), and it is a significantly better shape than the formations explained in the previous Tatefudori chapters.

「図２」


By the time we reach "Figure 2," Gote's formation is extremely solid and well-shaped, making it difficult for Sente to find an opening for an attack. This is precisely the ideal anti-Hineri-bisha formation Gote aimed for by delaying ▽３四歩 in the opening. At this stage, even if Sente tries to attack with ▲６五歩, the sequence ▽同歩, ▲同銀, ▽７七角成, ▲同飛, ▽６五銀 results in a "two-piece exchange" that favors Gote due to their stable castle. Because this defensive formation was so effective and caused the winning percentage of Hineri-bisha to drop, a different tactical approach was developed.

「図３」

Playing ▲５六歩 has become a powerful and common method for countering Gote's solid Kanemino (Gold Mino) castle. The key to this formation is first moving the King to ▲２八玉. Leaving the King at 3-9 is dangerous, as it remains vulnerable to checks along the opponent's Bishop diagonal.

Furthermore, bringing the Silvers to the 3-7 formation creates a defense known as "Gingasane Mino" (Double Silver Mino). This is an exceptionally sturdy fortification that reinforces a standard Kata-Mino (Partial Mino) by stacking two Silvers vertically on the same file.

The following sequence illustrates one of the primary offensive goals: repositioning the Bishop to ▲５七角, shifting the Rook to the 8th file, and targeting Gote's weakened right wing. This plan successfully breaks through the 8th file.

Please take special note of the edge attack starting from ▲９五歩. In that sequence:

  • If Gote does not take ▲９三歩, Sente follows with ▲９五香.
  • If Gote does not take the subsequent ▲９二歩, Sente plays ▲９一歩成.

In both cases, Sente gains a clear advantage.

A pawn dropped in a position where it can promote on the very next move, such as these, is called a "Tare-fu" (Dangling Pawn). This is a crucial tesuji (tactical maneuver) used in many different game situations.
