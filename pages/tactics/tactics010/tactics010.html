Yagura Strategy – Piece Development

As introduced in the opening patterns section, Shogi strategies are broadly divided into two categories: Ibisha (Static Rook) and Furibisha (Ranging Rook). I will now explain one representative strategy for each, following the three major principles of piece development, divided into a "Development Phase" and a "Combat Phase."

Since these are full-fledged authentic strategies, they may be slightly difficult for beginners. However, there is no need to understand every single tactic or nuance at this stage. It is more than enough for now to simply grasp the general flow of how the pieces are developed and how the fighting typically unfolds.

First, let's begin with the Yagura (Fortress) strategy, which was introduced in Opening Pattern 2 as the representative formation for Static Rook.

Yagura is one of the strategies in Shogi with the most variations. Even in the sequence leading to "Figure 1," many branches can occur; however, this specific position is a standard progression for a Ai-yagura (Double Yagura) game and is frequently seen in professional practice.

From here, the possibilities are virtually infinite, but let's look at one example of the piece development sequence required to complete the Yagura formation.

Although the number of moves from the start to this point is long, please remember the completed shape of the King, regardless of the intermediate steps. This formation, which provides an exceptionally solid defense against the opponent's strongest piece, the Rook, is known as the "Yagura-gakoi" (Fortress Castle). While there are many variations for the attacking side, the method of building the King's castle generally follows this kind of construction, though the order of moves may vary slightly. Please try to memorize the process of completing the castle.

I would like to add one supplementary note: during the sequence, both players pushed their edge pawns with ▲1六歩, ▽9四歩, ▲9六歩, and ▽1四歩. While pushing the edge pawn of the castle is usually beneficial because it gives the King more space, in Yagura, there are actually many attacking lines that involve the edge. Therefore, it is quite common not to respond when the opponent pushes their edge pawn. In this specific position, since neither side is in a shape to immediately attack the edge, you can consider the responses as a standard defensive measure. However, in many cases during the opening, it is better not to respond to an edge pawn push. I will show you an actual example of the reason for this later.
