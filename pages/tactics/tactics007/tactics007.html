Climbing Silver (Bogin) with Aigakari

　

Starting from this session, I will introduce positions where the core objectives of various strategies are executed perfectly.

We will begin with the Bōgin (Climbing Silver) strategy, which can be considered the absolute fundamental of Shogi tactics. This strategy has several variations, including the Aigakari type, Kakugawari (Bishop Exchange) type, and Anti-Furibisha (Anti-Ranging Rook) type. For this introduction, I will show you an ideal successful position for Climbing Silver within the Aigakari style, where both players advance their Rook pawns against each other.

From the Aigakari opening (Chapter 1) in the early stage, Sente chooses to retreat the Rook deeply to ▲２八飛, and the strategy of advancing the Silver directly up the Rook's file is called Bōgin (Climbing Silver).
In fact, this "Figure 1" is already a winning position for Sente.

Even if Gote attempts to repel the Silver that has advanced to 2-4 by dropping ▽２三歩, Sente has more pieces controlling the 2-3 square (the Rook and Silver) than Gote (the Gold). Therefore, Sente can simply play ▲同銀成. Following ▽同金 and ▲同飛成, the game reaches Figure 2, where Sente has created a Dragon at the head of the opponent's Bishop, resulting in a winning position.

This is the ideal position where the Climbing Silver attack has succeeded. Completely defending against this objective is surprisingly difficult. This sequence of retreating the Rook to 2-8 and advancing the Silver to 3-6 was previously called "Primitive Climbing Silver" and was considered a strategy exclusively for beginners. However, it has recently been played among professionals as well. Of course, in professional games, this ideal attack is not allowed to succeed so easily; instead, they use the threat of this objective to restrict the opponent's piece development.
