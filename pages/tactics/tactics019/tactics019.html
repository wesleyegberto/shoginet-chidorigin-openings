Masuda-style Ishida [2]

　

In this chapter, we will take a look at the powerful attacking lines that define the core objective of the Masuda-style Ishida.
Figure 1

Figure 1 is the same position as Figure 3 from the previous chapter. For the sequence of moves leading up to this ▲9六角 drop, please refer to the previous chapter. Regarding Sente's King position, it is a variation of the Mino castle where one Gold is missing, commonly referred to as a "Kata-Mino" (Partial Mino) castle. Although it only uses two pieces (one Silver and one Gold) for protection, it remains a sufficiently solid formation.

The immediate goal of this ▲9六角 drop is to capture the pawn at 8-5 that Gote advanced earlier. First, let's look at what happens if Gote tries to prevent this.

Moves from Figure 1 to Figure 2:

  • ▲8五桂
  • ▽同　角
  • ▲8六飛
  • ▽9六角
  • ▲8二飛成

Figure 2

Dropping ▽9四角 is the only way to protect the pawn at 8-5, but Sente captures it anyway with ▲8五桂. After ▽同角, Sente follows up with the excellent move ▲8六飛, swinging the Rook over.

Following this, a total exchange of Rooks and Bishops occurs. In the resulting position at Figure 2, Gote has gained a Knight, but while Sente's camp has no openings for a Rook or Bishop drop, Gote's formation is disorganized and full of weaknesses—such as the ▲6二飛 drop forking the King, Gold, and Silver. Thus, Sente holds the advantage.

Next, let's look at what happens if Gote does not defend the 8-5 pawn and instead continues to develop their castle to reinforce their camp.

Moves from Figure 1 to Figure 3:

  • ▽3三銀
  • ▲8五角
  • ▽4二金
  • ▲8六飛
  • ▽1四歩
  • ▲6三角成
  • ▽8六飛
  • ▲7二馬
Figure 3

Sente moves with ▲8五角 followed by ▲8六飛, then captures the Silver with ▲6三角成, sacrificing the Bishop. This is the core attacking sequence aimed for ever since the ▲9六角 drop in the Masuda-style Ishida.

Gote’s only real response is to initiate a Rook exchange with ▽8六飛. However, the critical point is that Sente does not take the Rook with ▲同歩; instead, Sente plays ▲7二馬, capturing the Gold. This results in an exchange of a Rook for three pieces: a Gold, a Silver, and a Pawn. Since there is a Shogi proverb that says, "If you can trade for two pieces, do so even for a pawn," this three-piece exchange is a massive material gain. Furthermore, Gote's Rook remains under attack (maintaining the initiative), making the position in Figure 3 overwhelmingly favorable for Sente.
