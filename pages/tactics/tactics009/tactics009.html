Three Major Principles of Piece Development

We have looked at Climbing Silver and Central Rook—strategies you are almost certain to encounter when learning Shogi. These two examples are ultra-aggressive "fast-attack" (kyusen) methods. It would be wonderful if one could always win so easily, but Shogi is not quite that simple.
In authentic strategic formations and piece development, there are basic principles for building a "good shape."

Looking at "Figure 1," which player do you think has the better shape—Sente or Gote? If you feel Sente's formation is superior, you have good intuition. Sente has fulfilled all three major principles of Shogi. Conversely, Gote has failed to meet these principles in every respect. So, what are these three major principles?

  1. Keep the Rook and King on opposite sides.
  2. Use three Gold and Silver pieces to castle the King.
  3. Attack with the Rook, Bishop, Silver, and Knight (and sometimes the Lance).
Figure 1

First, regarding Principle 1: Since the area around the Rook—your primary attacking force—naturally becomes the main battlefield, having the King nearby makes it a hindrance. This makes it difficult to fight and effectively halves your offensive potential.

Next, Principle 2 refers to the "Castle," which acts as a fortress to protect your most important piece—the King—from the opponent's attack, since losing the King (having no escape) means losing the game. Sente's defensive formation in "Figure 1" is the Mino-gakoi (Mino Castle). This is the most commonly used castle for Ranging Rook strategies and is an exceptionally solid and excellent formation.

Finally, Principle 3 concerns the ideal offensive setup. It is achieved when you can deploy your two strongest major pieces (Rook and Bishop), the Silver piece not used in the castle, and the Knight on the Rook's side (and sometimes the Lance) into attacking positions. Sente's offensive lineup in "Figure 1" is a development of the Sangen-bisha (Third-file Rook)—where the Rook is moved to the third file from the left—known specifically as the Ishida-ryu (Ishida Style). This is considered an ideal formation because it allows for the full utilization of the Rook, Bishop, Silver, and Knight.

Regarding Principles 2 and 3, while there are many exceptions concerning the specific distribution and types of pieces used, please understand that this composition is fundamentally considered "good shape." Based on these points, "Figure 1" is described as a game where Sente can play comfortably (it is easy to play or advantageous). When such a superior position is established before any pieces have actually clashed, it is referred to as a Sakusen-gachi (strategic win) or Komagumi-gachi (win by piece development). Finally, I will introduce two examples of "good shape" that serve as exceptions to Principles 2 and 3.

Figure 2

Sente's strategy is known as Ibisha Anaguma (Static Rook Anaguma), used against Ranging Rook. This is a strategy that uses four Gold and Silver pieces for the castle, compensating for the reduction in attacking force with incredibly tough defensive power.

Gote's strategy is a full-fledged Central Rook tactic called Tsuno-gin Nakabisha (Horned Silver Central Rook). In this formation, the Gold and Silver pieces are split between the left and right, which weakens the immediate castle around the King, but the setup features excellent overall defensive coverage and a balanced, gapless defense.

Both of these are quite advanced strategies. Since they may be difficult for beginners to master, please focus on Principles 2 and 3 as your foundation for now.
