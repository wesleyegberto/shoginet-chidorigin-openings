Primitive Central Rook (Genshi Nakabisha)

Among the Ranging Rook (Furibisha) strategies mentioned in the 5th opening pattern, the formation where the Rook is moved to the central file, 5八 for Sente or 5二 for Gote, is called the "Nakabisha" (Central Rook) strategy.

This time, we will look at a version of Central Rook suitable for beginners called "Genshi Nakabisha" (Primitive Central Rook). This strategy deviates from the six basic patterns, as it involves swinging the Rook immediately on the very first move.

After swinging the Rook to ▲5八, the Silver advances in front of the Rook—similar to the Bougin (Climbing Silver) strategy. Gote also moves to defend the center, and as a result of the collision between the Silvers, we arrive at the position in "Figure 1." In this situation, Sente already has a decisive advantage.

Starting with ▲5四歩 and dropping the Silver onto 5三, Sente successfully breaks through the 5th file as shown in "Figure 2," resulting in a decisive victory. This tactic of dropping pieces directly onto the opponent's pawns is colloquially known as a "heavy move" (omoi te); while such moves don't always work, here ▲5三銀 is a check, which prevents Gote from defending with a pawn drop like ▽5二歩打, making the attack successful.

Moving the Rook to ▲5八 (or ▽5二 for Gote) on the very first move—or pushing the 5th file pawn first and then switching to Central Rook on the second move—was once considered a strategy exclusively for beginners, much like the Bougin (Climbing Silver) mentioned previously. However, recently, these types of moves have begun to appear in professional play as well.

Of course, the professional versions are entirely different from this simple example of advancing the Silver without even opening the Bishop's diagonal. It is certainly more common to reach this strategy starting from ▲7六歩, but the fact that these moves are played at all by pros was almost unthinkable in the past. We will save the introduction of these modern "New-style Central Rook" strategies for another time.


The Genshi Nakabisha (Primitive Central Rook) shown here, along with the Aigakari Bougin (Double Wing Climbing Silver) from last time, are strategies that almost everyone who has just learned Shogi has either played or played against at least once. Whether it is the Climbing Silver targeting the "round head" of the Bishop (the square directly in front of it where it cannot move), or the Central Rook targeting the King—the ultimate goal—from the very beginning, both strategies are logical and have easy-to-understand objectives, which makes them very popular.
