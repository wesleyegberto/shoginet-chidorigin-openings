Masuda-style Ishida [1]

As the second part of our series on strategies that evolve from the Ishida Style into different objectives, I will explain the "Masuda-style Ishida" strategy. This tactic was originated by the 4th Lifetime Meijin, Kozo Masuda.

In the Quick Ishida (Haya-ishida), we attempted a super-rapid attack with ▲7四歩 in response to Gote's 8th move ▽6二銀. However, we saw that if the opponent responds optimally, the results aren't as successful as one might hope, even if the position isn't necessarily bad.

Therefore, we pass on the immediate attack and instead play ▲3八玉, moving the King to aim for the Mino castle.

Gote's ▽6四歩 is a move intended to prevent ▲7四歩. With this, even if Sente plays ▲7四歩, the 7th file is defended via ▽同歩 ▲同飛 ▽6三銀. I will show this position in Figure 2; please memorize it as a standard defensive formation.

In response to ▽6四歩, Sente plays ▲7六飛, floating the Rook. This move forces Gote to make a choice regarding the Bishop exchange. If Gote plays a move like ▽6三銀 instead of ▽8八角成, Sente can jump the Knight with ▲7七桂 and aim for the "Full Ishida" formation shown in Figure 1 of Chapter 9. In other words, if Gote wants to prevent Sente from completing the ideal Ishida; formation, they have no choice but to exchange Bishops here.

Figure 2

After that, Gote's move of ▽2二銀 is a preparation to defend with ▽3三銀 against Sente's potential ▲3六飛, which would target the pawn at 3-4. From there, both sides continue to reinforce their King positions. A key point in the sequence leading up to Figure 1 is that Sente plays ▲7八金 and Gote plays ▽7二金, moving their Golds to the side opposite the King. When major pieces (Rook and Bishop) are exchanged in the opening, this is done to ensure no gaps are left in the home camp. For Gote, this also serves to reinforce the 7th file; this setup can be considered a standard defensive formation against the Ishida style.

Now, both sides appear to have solid formations with no obvious moves, but here comes the signature move of the Masuda-style Ishida. This is shown in Figure 3.
