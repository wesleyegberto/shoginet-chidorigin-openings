Double Wing Attack - Vertical Pawn Take (Tatefudori) [2]

The Tatefudori strategy is also known by the name Hineri-bisha (Twisting Rook), which is the more common term today. However, since a Twisting Rook setup can be reached from openings other than Tatefudori, this chapter will focus on explaining it specifically as the Tatefudori strategy.
Figure 1


　

Figure 1 is the same position as Figure 3 from the previous chapter. Please refer to the previous chapter for the sequence of moves leading up to this point.

From the initial Aigakari (Double Wing Attack) opening, Sente retreats the Rook to the 2-6 square to prevent Gote from exchanging the Rook's pawn. This formation is known as Uki-bisha (Floating Rook), a setup that leverages Sente's opening advantage.

Gote, unable to exchange their Rook's pawn, develops with ▽６二銀. (While moving to 7-2 is more common nowadays, we will proceed with the explanation using this variation). When Gote opens the Bishop's diagonal with ▽３四歩, Sente swings the Rook to 3-6 to target that pawn—this maneuver is the origin of the name Tatefudori (Vertical Pawn Take).

Gote defends with ▽３三金. This might make the Rook's move seem ineffective at first glance, but in reality, capturing the pawn is not the true objective. Furthermore, if Gote were to defend with ▽８四飛 instead of the Gold, Sente could play ▲２七銀 and advance the Silver in a Bōgin (Climbing Silver) style. In that scenario, Gote would be unable to push their pawns on the 7th through 4th files and their Rook would be cramped, resulting in a favorable position for Sente.

Sente pushes the edge pawn with ▲９六歩 and then opens the Bishop’s diagonal with ▲７六歩. After this, if Sente were to play ▲７七角, Gote would no longer be able to exchange their Rook's pawn, so Gote pushes ▽８六歩. Following ▲同歩 and ▽同飛, Sente immediately plays ▲７五歩—this is the primary objective of the Tatefudori strategy. In other words, by having the Rook positioned at 3-6 to provide support, Sente forces the opponent into a Rook exchange.

A Rook exchange in this formation is disadvantageous for Gote; while Sente’s camp has no gaps to drop a Rook, Gote’s formation is prone to creating weaknesses. This is similar to Rook exchanges found in rapid-attack variations of Ranging Rook vs. Static Rook. Gote is forced to retreat the Rook deeply to ▽８二飛, at which point jumping the Knight with ▲７七桂 is the next crucial move of the Tatefudori strategy.

Please be careful: if the edge pawn at 9-6 had not been pushed, Gote could drop ▽８七歩, capturing either the Bishop or the Gold. Furthermore, if Gote plays ▽８六歩 after ▲７七桂 aiming for ▽８七歩成, Sente can drop ▲８五歩 and then play ▲８六飛 to take the pawn. Gote, being out of pawns, would be unable to defend the 8th file, resulting in a winning advantage for Sente.

And then comes the surprising ▲９七角. Some may think that Gote can promote their Rook with ▽８九飛成 in "Figure 3," but this is actually the next objective of the Tatefudori strategy. The continuation will be explained in the next chapter.
