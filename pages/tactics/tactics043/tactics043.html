The castle completed by ▽４一玉, ▽５一金, and ▽６二銀 is known as the Nakahara Style or Nakahara Castle. Compared to the Nakazumai formation, it is sturdier than it looks, and its defining feature is the ability to launch an offensive from the 5th file. In the ▽５二玉 formation, attacking from the center would expose the King's head to danger; this setup avoids that risk.

The sequence up to Figure 2 is an example of what happens when Sente plays defensively. Gote utilizes the right Knight with ▽７三桂 and maintains an offensive pressure across a wide range from the 8th to the 5th files. In Figure 2, the evaluation is still even and the real battle is just beginning, but in a practical match, the attacking side (Gote) often finds the position easier to win.

Against Gote's offensive from the 5th file, it is effective for Sente to play ▲３六歩 immediately after moving ▲５八玉, targeting the head of the Bishop at ３三. In response to ▽５四歩, Sente exchanges Bishops and plays ▲３五歩 to apply pressure to the head of the Knight. If Sente fails to exchange Bishops, Gote will play ▽５五歩, clearing the horizontal path for their Rook. If Gote plays ▽６二銀 instead of ▽５四歩, Sente will play ▲３五歩, preventing the 5th file pawn from advancing; thus, Gote has no time to complete their castle.

In this variation, playing ▽７四歩 is met with ▲３四歩, which prevents Gote's right Knight from joining the attack. Conversely, many gaps for Bishop drops open up in Gote’s camp, making the situation in Figure 3 advantageous for Sente.

The Nakahara Style is yet another variation of the Aerial Battle strategy with numerous branches for both sides and no definitive conclusion. However, if Gote could move their Rook more freely, they could unleash even greater offensive power while making better use of their sturdy King formation. It was from this very inspiration that a new strategy was born—one that would rewrite the history of Yoko-fudori and Shogi itself.
