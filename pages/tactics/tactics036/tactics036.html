Against Gote's ▽５四香, the ▲８五飛 drop is the best move and effectively shuts down Gote's options. At this point, playing ▽５七香成 is futile due to the ▲５八歩 defensive drop. If a ▽８八飛 drop worked here, Gote would be winning; however, ▲８五飛 prevents this while simultaneously aiming for ▲８一飛成. Gote plays ▽４五桂 and Sente responds with ▲同飛, forcing the Rook to change positions and preventing the immediate ▲８一飛成, but now the Horse at １一 protects ８八. In Figure 2, Sente holds the advantage.

I would like to say this concludes the variation, but within this sequence, there is a type of trap where Gote plays ▽５五香 instead of ▽５四香, which I will explain.

If Gote plays ▽５五香 and Sente responds with ▲８五飛 (as in the ▽５四香 variation), Gote has the counter-drop of ▽２五飛. If Sente then plays ▲８一飛成, Gote's ▽２八飛成 is severe and Sente falls into a disadvantage. Therefore, Sente must defend with ▲２七歩, but Gote then plays ▽５七香不成 (Check), Sente defends with ▲５八歩, and Gote captures the Rook with ▽８五飛. This type of tactic is called "sunuki" (an uncontested capture of a major piece behind another), as seen in Figure 3.

Figure 3

As I have mentioned before, there is no meaning in simply memorizing tactical sequences (tesuji). You must be able to adapt when your opponent changes their moves. Against this ▽５五香, the ▲６六角 drop—which was a blunder against ▽５四香—becomes the correct move. This is because, unlike the ▽５四香 variation, Sente can respond to ▽４五桂 with ▲５五馬, capturing the Lance.
